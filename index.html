The Last Train Home

scribbled june 18 · scored to "Cigarettes out the Window"

The station at Maranhalli closed in 1991, but my father kept its timetable folded in his shirt pocket until the day he died. He could recite it the way other men recite prayer — the 6:40 to the city, the 9:15 that only ran on Sundays, the last train home at a quarter past eleven.

I used to think it was nostalgia. I understand now it was something closer to faith.

"you always wait for the things you love. even after they stop coming."

The platform is still there, if you know where the road forks past the dry well. The rails were lifted decades ago, but the gravel keeps their shape — two pale lines running off into the tall grass like the memory of a sentence you can almost finish.

He went there every evening. Took out the timetable. Checked his watch against a train that hadn't arrived in thirty years. Neighbours called it grief. I called it stubbornness. We were both wrong.

On the last evening I followed him. Sat down without a word. And at exactly a quarter past eleven I heard it — faint, impossible, the long exhale of a train slowing into a station that no longer existed. He smiled, folded the timetable, and stood as if to board.
